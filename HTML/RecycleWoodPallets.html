Introduction

        This report is based on the idea that Arcelormittal wants to utilize its waste products and become a more environmentally friendly company. For example, in this specific report we will focus on improving the use of wood pallets used to ship products to Arcelormittal’s company. By the time the pallets arrive to the company they do not know the best possible solution for dealing with them. It is mandatory that the process being implemented will abide to the companies needs and wishes. For example, the number one wish from the company is to make sure that the process has a closed loop. This means that the process needs to be a continuous cycle that never ends. Luckily, the design made will give a great representation of a possible solution to Arcelormittal's dilemma.

        The design has a couple very important steps that it needs to follow to become a closed loop cycle. First, Arcelormittal has to obtain and collect all the wood pallets that are sent to them. Then from there, the pallets would be separated between the old pallets and the newer pallets. This is done because the newer pallets would be turned into furniture. After the furniture becomes very run down, like the older pallets, both will be transformed into the environmentally friendly wood fibre insulation. After the insulation reaches its shelf life it would go through the process of becoming paper. This is where the loop is formed due to the fact that the paper made can be recycled over and over again.

        This process is economically and environmentally friendly. It provides a great way to make newer profits besides the steel products already being sold. Not only this, but the process itself will successfully close the loop and easily abide by the customer needs.

Problematic Areas

The first problem we encountered was “closing the loop.” We were able to think linearly, with ideas of what types of products to make out of reusable wood. These ideas included things like furniture, a playground, flooring, instruments, and hockey sticks. However, we were faced with the question of what to do when these products were no longer usable.

One idea we had was to turn wood pallets into wood foam, which could be used for packaging. However, since the production of this wood foam is an up-and-coming technique, there was not enough research on the recyclability, cost, etc. of the material. Another issue we faced was we could not find information on how many times packing peanuts could be recycled. After going back to square one, we found that instead of wood foam, wood pallets could be used to make wood fiber insulation, which would be more beneficial since it lasts longer than packing peanuts. Additionally, we know that we can produce paper from wood fiber, which can be recycled over and over.

Paper manufacturing includes the processing of wood, recovered paper and paperboard, and other cellulose fibers into thousands of end-use products. There are multiple methods that can be used to turn insulation into paper. Two of these processes are mechanical pulping, which grinds raw wood into pulp, and chemical pulping, which dissolves away the dark compounds in the wood. Paper mills differ in their processes based on the source of fiber used and the end product produced. There are three basic types of mills.

Pulp mills make pulp, which is a mixture of cellulose fibers and water used as the basis of all paper products. Pulp is made in several ways, depending on the type of paper being produced. Wood chips can be chemically or mechanically separated into individual wood fibers in a process called pulping. In chemical pulping, the wood chips are “cooked” in a digester at an elevated pressure with an appropriate solution of chemicals to dissolve the lignin (the “glue” that binds the fibers in the wood) and allow the cellulose fiber bundles in the wood to separate into individual cellulose fibers. Since chemical processing is gentle on the cellulose fiber, chemical pulps tend to have longer fibers and make strong paper such as printing and writing papers and paperboard. In mechanical pulping, chemicals are not used to remove the lignin in the wood chips. Instead, wood chips are pressed against a grinder that physically separates the fibers. Mechanical pulps have shorter fiber lengths and produce papers which do not require as much strength, such as newsprint. After the fibers have been separated, the mill washes and de-contaminates the pulp. The bleached or unbleached wood pulp is then pumped onto rolling wire screen mats that vibrate slightly to allow water to drain out of the pulp and to help the fibers interlock into sheets. By varying the amount of pulp pumped onto the rolling mats, the speed of the mat, and the speed of the vibrations, paper with different qualities and properties is produced. The sheets then pass through a long series of rollers that press out any remaining moisture, followed by steam-heated drums that dry the paper. Lastly, the sheets are polished and wrinkles are smoothed in a process called calendaring.

At recycled paper processing mills, the recovered paper is combined with water in a large vessel called a pulper. The pulper acts like a blender to separate fibers in the paper sheets from each other. The resultant slurry then passes through screens and other separation processes to remove contaminants such as ink, clays, dirt, plastic and metals. The amount of contaminants that are acceptable in the pulp depends upon the type of paper being produced.

Some mills use both recycled and virgin fiber to make paper. These mills are typically set up to process virgin wood into pulp and incorporate recovered fiber by buying bales of recycled pulp which are added to the wood pulp.

Papermaking is a very technical process that requires energy, water, wood fiber and other materials to make a finished product. Within this production stage there are many opportunities for manufacturers to address environmental performance issues, including climate change, efficient use of recovered fiber, utilizing self-generated energy and more.

As a consumer, it is very important to have a reasonable understanding of what manufacturers are doing to increase their environmental performance at this critical stage of the paper life cycle. The production stage illustrates very clearly that informed environmental decisions consider multiple attributes rather than single attributes. Fiber is the single largest component in the manufacturing and use of paper and pulp products. With ever increasing demand, the efficient use of wood fiber is a critical issue. For example, the use of recycled fiber can reduce the demand for fresh fiber from forests and avoid greenhouse gas emissions.

Consumers use paper to meet many different needs and each requires different and specific properties, such as strength, brightness and absorbency. Blends of both fresh and recycled fibers, in varying proportions, are being used to deliver the required properties to the consumer. While it is technically impossible to sustain society’s long-term paper needs without freshfiber, no reusable paper should go to a landfill.

The final step of the recycling process is to recycle paper into paper, and that is how our close loop built. Since paper is a easily recycled material and can be recycled repeatedly several times, it is a close loop which can produce economical profits many times.          

Below is the process to recycle paper into paper:

a)Paper is taken from the bin and deposited in a large recycling container along with paper from other recycling bins.

b)The paper is taken to a recycling plant where it is separated into types and grades.

c)The separated paper is then washed with soapy water to remove inks, plastic film, staples and glue. The paper is put into a large holder where it is mixed with water to create ‘slurry’.

d)By adding different materials to the slurry, different paper products can be created, such as cardboard, newsprints or office paper.

e)The slurry is spread using large rollers into large thin sheets.

f)The paper is left to dry, and then it is rolled up ready to be cut and sent back to the shops.

g)The used paper that people throw into the recycling bins will be recollected and goes to the first step of paper recycling.

        Recycling paper instead of landfill it have several benefits including conserves natural resources, saves energy, reduces greenhouse gas emissions, and keeps landfill space free for other types of trash that can't be recycled. Recycling paper preserves trees and forests. Every ton of recycled paper saves about 17 trees. It can effectively protect the forest from being overcut. Moreover, Making recycled paper pulp consumes less energy and water compared to generating paper pulp directly from trees. Recycling one ton of paper saves energy equivalent to the energy needed to power the average U.S. home for six months and saves about 7,000 gallons of water. In addition, Recycling paper reduces methane and carbon dioxide in the atmosphere. Paper produces the gas methane which contributes to global climate change when it is in the landfill. Trees absorb carbon dioxide but when they are cut down to make paper products, more carbon dioxide is released than absorbed. According to the EPA, recycling one ton of paper can reduce greenhouse gas levels by one metric ton of carbon equivalent. Last but not least, Paper makes up about 28 percent of solid trash in landfills and one ton of paper takes up about 3.3 cubic yards of landfill space, according to the EPA. If we recycle the paper instead of landfill it, the space used to landfill paper can be used to landfill other materials that can not be recycle.
